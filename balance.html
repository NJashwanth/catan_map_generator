The Math of a Fair Board

Why 6s and 8s decide games, and how spacing them out keeps things interesting.

Two Dice Are Not Fair — and That's the Game

Rolling two six-sided dice produces 36 equally likely combinations, but the sums are anything but equal. There is exactly one way to roll a 2 (1+1) and one way to roll a 12 (6+6), but five ways each to roll a 6 or an 8. A hex numbered 6 will pay out five times as often as a hex numbered 2. Every number token shows this as a row of dots — "pips" — one pip per combination:

  • 2 and 12 — 1 pip each (about a 3% chance per roll)
  • 3 and 11 — 2 pips (6%)
  • 4 and 10 — 3 pips (8%)
  • 5 and 9 — 4 pips (11%)
  • 6 and 8 — 5 pips (14%), shown in red
  • 7 — 6 pips, but 7 moves the robber instead of producing resources

Counting Pips Around a Corner

A settlement sits on a corner touching up to three hexes, so its expected income is simply the sum of the pips around it. A corner touching a 6, an 8, and a 9 collects on 14 of 36 combinations — nearly 40% of all rolls. A corner touching a 2, a 3, and a 12 collects on just 4 of 36. When you evaluate starting positions, ignore the printed numbers and count pips; it's the single most useful habit a new player can learn.

Why Adjacent 6s and 8s Break Boards

When two five-pip hexes share an edge, the three corners along that edge become dramatically stronger than anything else on the map. Whoever places there first banks a statistical advantage for the whole game, and the remaining players fight over scraps. The generator's fair number spacing option rejects any layout where a 6 or 8 neighbors another 6 or 8, which caps how strong any single corner can be without flattening the board into uniformity — there are still better and worse spots, they're just spread around the island.

Resource Variety Matters Too

Numbers aren't the whole story. If all four forest hexes end up in one corner of the island, one player can monopolize wood and everyone else must trade through them. The reduce resource clumping option limits how many same-type hexes can sit side by side, so every region of the board offers a mix. Combined with number spacing, this produces boards where several distinct strategies — ore-wheat cities, wood-brick roads, sheep-heavy development cards — are viable from different starting areas.

Harbors Change the Value of Everything

A 2:1 harbor effectively doubles the value of overflow production in its resource. A pasture-heavy board with a sheep harbor turns "too much sheep" from a joke into an engine. When you evaluate a generated board, glance at which 2:1 harbors landed near strong matching hexes — those pairings are usually the most contested placements after the obvious high-pip corners.

Balanced Isn't Boring

A perfectly uniform board would be a coin flip; a wildly skewed one is decided during setup. The sweet spot — and what this generator aims for — is a board where the best positions are debatable. If your group prefers chaos, switch both fairness checks off and take the island as the dice intended. Either way, the seed system means any board you love (or love to argue about) can be regenerated and replayed — see the FAQ for how seeds work.
