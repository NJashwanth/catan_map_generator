Setting Up a Game, Step by Step

From an empty table to first placements in about ten minutes.

What Goes on the Table

A classic 3–4 player island is built from 19 terrain hexes: four forest (wood), four pasture (sheep), four fields (wheat), three hills (brick), three mountains (ore), and one desert. Around the island sits a coastline — in the boxed game it's assembled from frame pieces — carrying nine harbors: four generic 3:1 harbors and one 2:1 harbor for each of the five resources. Eighteen number tokens (two each of 3–6 and 8–11, one each of 2 and 12) cover every land hex except the desert.

Step 1 — Lay the Island

Arrange the terrain hexes in rows of 3-4-5-4-3. If you're using the generator, simply copy the on-screen layout tile by tile, working row by row from the top. Sorting your physical tiles into stacks by terrain type first makes this much faster — most groups can rebuild a generated board in two or three minutes.

Step 2 — Place the Number Tokens

Match each hex's number token to the generated board. The desert never gets a token — instead, the robber starts there. If a generated board ever feels too spiky, check the board balance guide for how the dots on each token translate into expected income, and consider regenerating with the fairness options enabled.

Step 3 — Add the Harbors

Place harbors around the coast to match the generator's port ring: 3:1 harbors let you trade any three identical resource cards to the bank for one card of your choice, while 2:1 harbors upgrade that trade for one specific resource. Harbor positions matter more than new players expect — a 2:1 harbor adjacent to a strong matching hex is often the difference between a good board position and a winning one.

Step 4 — First Placements

Each player places one settlement and one adjoining road in turn order, then a second settlement and road in reverse order (the "snake draft"). You collect starting resources from the hexes around your second settlement, so it usually pays to place your first settlement for long-term production and your second for immediate resources and road-building materials.

Playing with 5 or 6 Players

The extension stretches the island to 30 hexes in rows of 3-4-5-6-5-4-3, adds a second desert, brings the token count to 28, and expands the coastline to 11 harbors (five 3:1, six 2:1 — sheep appears twice). Two extra rules keep big games moving: the special building phase lets every player build (but not trade) after each turn, and many groups raise the victory target to 12 points so the endgame stays competitive. Select "5 players" or "6 players" in the generator and it handles the layout, token mix, and harbor count automatically.

House-Rule Friendly

The generator's options map to common table rules: the friendly robber option reflects the popular house rule that the robber can't target players until everyone has more than two points, and the victory point selector accommodates groups who play to 8 for a fast game or 12+ for a long one. However you play, the goal of a generated board is the same — skip the setup debate and get to the first dice roll sooner.
